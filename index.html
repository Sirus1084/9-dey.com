The bedrock

Health Tech Founder Thesis

Why I'm doing this, and what the durable edge actually is.

In health tech, the durable edge is built on a foundation of knowing how the work is done — clinical and scientific depth — and then compounding it: knowing which business models work, holding the relationships and insider access, choosing a domain regulated enough that depth itself is scarce, and pairing all of it with a technical co-founder who can build.

01

The reframe

Clinical and scientific knowledge is the foundation — and it keeps deepening, not just from having trained, but from staying immersed and proactive: reading, and talking to the people I know from med school who are still inside it. On its own, though, that knowledge is necessary, not sufficient. The edge is that foundation plus four things stacked: fluency in business models — what's investable, what's been tried, where the money moves; relationships with the people who buy, deploy and decide; insider access to the institutions; and depth in a domain regulated enough that the depth is rare. The knowledge is the floor; the terrain is the edge.

02

Why now

When the marginal cost of building software falls toward zero, "we built it" stops being a moat. Anything a small team can assemble in a weekend, another can rebuild the next. Value migrates to whatever doesn't get cheaper as code gets free. In consumer and non-regulated B2B SaaS, what survives is thin — distribution, brand, switching costs, all compressible. In healthcare, what survives is a thick stack cheap code can't touch.

This is the shift YC captured in "The Age Of The 40-Year-Old Solo Founder Is Here": when code is nearly free, the scarce thing is knowing what to build and having the standing to build it where it's hard. The advantage swings away from the dorm-room outsider and toward the operator who carries real domain depth and access into a regulated field — even if my own read is that the domain half still pairs best with a technical builder, not a true solo run.

03

The moat stack — what doesn't get cheaper

Regulatory clearanceMHRA SaMD classification, DCB0129/0160 clinical safety, DTAC — earned, not coded.
Clinical evidenceAn outcomes base a rival needs years and real deployments to replicate.
Data governance & accessIG, Caldicott, DPIAs, data-sharing agreements — plus proprietary data with a compounding loop.
Workflow entrenchmentIntegration into a clinical workflow that's painful and risky to rip out.
Liability & trustRisk-averse institutional buyers who move on credibility, not features.

As the build moat goes to zero, the relative value of every row rises. Standing in the most regulated, relationship-gated, evidence-heavy industry there is, as a credentialed insider, is a good place to stand.

04

The critical correction

Domain expertise is not the moat. It is what lets me see and build the moat.

Plenty of people know healthcare deeply; knowledge in my head is table stakes, not defensibility. The expertise earns its keep by letting me identify the defensible wedge outsiders can't perceive, then construct the clearance, the dataset with a loop, the sticky integration, the evidence. The honest claim is narrow and it's the bedrock: my domain depth lets me find and build moated things — it is not itself the moat.

And the depth isn't static — it's being refreshed at exactly the right moment. Healthcare has changed enormously since I left med school, and re-entering it in an AI-native era does two things at once: it deepens what I already know, and it lets me re-read the field through a commercial lens I didn't have the first time — the one I built through Studdle, Decrey and EF. That combination is rarer than either half alone. On EF selection day the signal from the room was consistent: people kept coming to me because of the health background — how much I knew clinically and scientifically. That's the raw material. The commercial layer is what turns it into an edge.

And the access isn't the kind consulting or adjacent roles can manufacture. Knowing people in US payer strategy, knowing directors of operations inside the NHS, holding those connections and that understanding — that's a different thing from the access a consulting engagement rents you for the length of a project. Health tech is a strange industry in that it demands dual vision: you have to underwrite both health ROI and commercial ROI at once. Health ROI I can read natively, because I was trained to. Commercial ROI needs a different lens and a different set of connections — and that's exactly what immersion in health-tech startups, consulting and the commercial world builds. The thesis is the marriage of the two: native health-ROI judgement, plus an acquired commercial-ROI lens and the relationships that feed it.

05

Pick versus build — two muscles

Picking the wedge rewards breadth and evaluative sharpness: what consulting and a wide commercial map provide. Building the moat is institutional-deployment craft — running a safety case through rejection and redraft, surviving NHS procurement, integrating against a real trust's EHR, standing up the evidence. That craft only forms against a real institutional counterparty, in a founding or operating seat, or it's hired onto the team. It can't be a side project, because the side project has no institution on the other side. Both muscles are required; they're acquired in different places.

06

Necessary, not sufficient

Regulation raises the floor and slows everyone equally; it doesn't, by itself, hand me an edge. Babylon had maximal insider access in the most regulated industry there is and still collapsed — access won contracts ahead of a validated product and sustainable economics. "Hard to enter" is not "moat." The moat is the specific defensible thing I construct.

The moat comes with a tax, and I'm choosing into it knowingly: longer cycles, heavier capital, a heavier evidence burden, slower iteration. The point of immersion is that I get to watch other health-tech companies and healthcare clients pay that tax and navigate it — not read about it, watch it. How do they manage cash flow across an eighteen-month cycle, and how do they pitch that timeline to investors? How do they actually assemble an evidence base, and what counts? I can research all of this online — Claude will give me the mechanics. But two things don't come from research: the relationships, which are the main prize of the consulting and operating world, and seeing it happen in the real world rather than on a page. Same learnings, different weight. It hits different when you've sat in the room while it happened.

07

Team composition

The winning pattern isn't the lone clinician. It's domain credibility + a technical/operator complement. Across 300+ health-tech unicorn founders, a slim majority were insiders, but the through-line was teams (80%+) pairing credibility with build capability, and seniority (70% had ten-plus years). My job isn't to be the whole company — it's to be the credible domain half and pair deliberately. That search runs constantly.

08

What this means for me

I commit to health as the 10–15 year domain on first-principles defensibility grounds, not preference alone. I use whichever vehicle I land — consulting or commercial — to sharpen the pick (business-model fluency, relationships, access) while I find the technical co-founder and the validated wedge that let me build. The institutional craft I don't yet have isn't a blocker: it's learnable inside my own company with the right co-founder and a real design partner, or hired onto the team. Founding stays the destination. The two paths are just the two ways in — and both keep it live.
